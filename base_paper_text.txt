ABSTRACT DevOps and microservice architecture (MSA) are central to modern software delivery, yet their
conceptual alignment remains fragmented across the literature. This study empirically analyzes how DevOps
practices correspond to MSA principles and how these relationships inform architectural and operational
decisions. From 84 peer-reviewed sources (2016–2025), we extract and normalize over 200 DevOps–MSA
concept pairs and organize them into a five-category taxonomy: deployment automation and orchestration,
observability and runtime diagnostics, scalability and modularity, security and governance, and configuration and environment management. To operationalize these mappings, we develop a retrieval-augmented
generation (RAG) framework that combines sentence-transformer embeddings with GPT-based reasoning
to generate bidirectional concept recommendations. Quantitative evaluation shows that RAG outperforms
lexical and embedding-only baselines by roughly 20 % in precision and ranking, while qualitative assessment
confirms semantic accuracy and contextual relevance. Deployment automation and observability emerge
as the most empirically supported categories, whereas governance-related links remain largely conceptual.
Industrial case studies in software, banking, and insurance sectors demonstrate measurable improvements
in release speed and developer satisfaction when the framework supports transformation planning. The
complete dataset and open-source RAG prototype are released to facilitate replication and extension. Overall,
the findings indicate that empirically grounded RAG enhances traceability and evidence-based decisionmaking between architecture and operations in complex software systems.

I. INTRODUCTION

In recent years, Development and Operations (DevOps) and
Microservice Architecture (MSA) have emerged as two of the
most influential paradigms in modern software engineering.
DevOps emphasizes automation, collaboration, and continuous integration and delivery (CI/CD), aiming to bridge the
gap between development and operations teams. In parallel, Microservice Architecture promotes the decomposition
of monolithic systems into loosely coupled, independently
deployable services. These paradigms have significantly
reshaped how software systems are designed, deployed, and
maintained.

II. BACKGROUND

The convergence of DevOps and microservice architecture
(MSA) has reshaped how modern systems are designed
and operated. DevOps emphasizes collaboration, automation, and continuous delivery, while MSA promotes modularity via independently deployable services. Although these
paradigms emerged from distinct motivations—operational
efficiency for DevOps and architectural flexibility for
MSA—their co-adoption is now common in both research
and practice [1], [2], [3], [4].
A. DEVOPS AND MSA: A CONVERGING TRAJECTORY

A substantial body of work highlights synergy between
DevOps and MSA in cloud-native contexts. Core DevOps
practices—containerization, CI/CD, infrastructure as code
(IaC), observability—act as operational enablers for
microservices deployment and lifecycle management [1], [2],
[3], [4]. Recent studies ground this synergy with concrete
stacks/workflows on Kubernetes that integrate CI/CD, monitoring, and automation [5]. Organizational evidence from a
cross-company ethnography documents how teams structure
around DevOps–MSA, including benefits and recurring friction points (tooling fragmentation, coordination overhead)
[6]. At the testing/quality layer, approaches that simplify
cluster-internal integration testing help reduce brittleness
of end-to-end tests and accelerate feedback [7]. Modeldriven development further strengthens traceability from
architecture to operations by deriving microservice code or
interlinking viewpoints with DevOps-enabled modeling languages [8], [9]. Security concerns are addressed in refactoring
paths from monoliths to microservices, where code-analysisdriven decomposition helps mitigate vulnerabilities during
partitioning [10]. The scope of DevOps–MSA also extends
to edge deployments [11], legacy system decomposition
via clustering [12], and ChatOps for developer–operations
workflows [13].
B. CONCEPTUAL MAPPING AND CO-DEPENDENCY

Several studies formalize how DevOps practices reinforce microservice design principles and vice versa—e.g.,
automated testing, deployment orchestration, and runtime
monitoring supporting service granularity, resilience, and
decentralized governance [14], [15]. DevSecOps metrics tailored for cloud-based web microservices connect pipeline
telemetry with quality attributes (security, reliability, scalability) [16]. Curriculum-oriented work argues for aligning DevOps tools and MSA principles in education [17].
AI is increasingly mapped across the microservices lifecycle
(decomposition, deployment automation, failure prediction,
compliance), indicating a trend toward intelligent, feedbackrich pipelines [18]. Complementing these, recent systematic
reviews and empirical studies provide broader evidence: a

JSS SLR synthesizing performance/scalability methods in
microservice systems [19]; an empirical JSS study quantifying performance overheads from code instrumentation in

C. EMPIRICAL STUDIES ON JOINT ADOPTION

Empirical accounts generally report improvements in deployment frequency, team autonomy, and operability when
DevOps and MSA are co-adopted [3], [4], [22]. Benefits
depend on balanced adoption: introducing MSA without
sufficient automation, observability, governance, or security
often leads to bottlenecks and cognitive load. Socio-technical
enablers (role clarity, cross-functional collaboration) and
inhibitors (siloed tooling, fragmented ownership) have been
observed in practice [2], [6], [23]. Energy-aware operations
and monitoring configurations also influence resource efficiency in microservice systems [24].
D. ROLE OF AI AND RAG IN DEVOPS-MSA SYSTEMS

Advances in large language models (LLMs) and retrievalaugmented generation (RAG) enable context-aware guidance that blends architectural knowledge with operational
practices. RAG can inject domain knowledge into model
outputs for decision support across architecture and operations [25]. Systematic mappings indicate growing use of AI
throughout the microservices lifecycle—service decomposition, deployment automation, failure prediction, and compliance enforcement—aligning with DevOps’ automation
and feedback ethos [13]. Human-in-the-loop assistants for
microservice development and operations provide complementary interaction patterns [13].
E. IDENTIFIED GAPS

Despite a maturing literature, holistic, empirically validated
frameworks for modeling DevOps–MSA co-dependencies at
scale remain limited. Many contributions focus on individual
phases (e.g., testing, monitoring) or specific technologies;
comparatively fewer provide reusable datasets, controlled
vocabularies/ontologies, or benchmarkedevaluations. Recent
work calls for stronger cross-cutting links among performance/scalability [19], observability trade-offs [20], and
QoS attributes [21]. Additional directions—e.g., energyaware operations [17], AI-guided migration/modeling [8],
[9], [26], governance and enterprise alignment [27], and
curriculum-level skill development [12]—remain underexplored from a systems perspective.
This study addresses these gaps by (i) curating a dataset
of DevOps–MSA concept pairs from 84 peer-reviewed publications, (ii) defining a controlled vocabulary to resolve
overlapping constructs, and (iii) proposing a RAG-based

recommendation framework that automates cross-domain
mapping and surfaces explainable, evidence-grounded suggestions.
III. DATASET CONSTRUCTION AND PREPARATION

To uncover conceptual relationships between DevOps and
MSA, we constructed a manually curated dataset based
on 84 publications (2016–2025). Sources were selected
via a structured screening of journals, conferences, workshops, theses, and book chapters. The dataset underpins both
our empirical analysis and the RAG-based recommendation
framework.

2) RAG SYSTEM

Following the dataset construction in Section III, we implement a five-stage pipeline (Figure 4):
1. Concept extraction & normalization. From the
84 screened publications, we extract domain terms and
normalize them to a controlled vocabulary (Ontology
v1.0) defined in §III-D (rules R1–R7).
2. Mapping construction. We build bidirectional
DevOps↔MSA mappings. Each mapping is linked to
its source(s), assigned a brief definition, and tagged
with evidencelevel (empirical vs conceptual).
3. Indexing for retrieval. Canonical concepts and short
definitions are embedded and indexed for similarity
search; a parallel lexical index supports keyword baselines.
4. RAG recommendation. For a query concept (DevOps
or MSA), we retrieve candidate concepts and synthesize a concise, evidence-grounded recommendation
with an LLM.
5. Evaluation. We compare RAG against non-RAG baselines with standard IR metrics and a domain-specific
efficiency metric (RAE@k), using double-annotated
judgments and inter-rater agreement.
The full dataset and RAG-based mapping tool are available
at GitHub repository1 and archived with DOI in Zenodo.2

A. INTERNAL VALIDITY
1) POTENTIAL BIAS IN CONCEPT EXTRACTION AND
MAPPING

Although mappings were manually curated and doubleannotated, subjective interpretation of terms (e.g., observability vs. logging) can introduce bias. We mitigated this
by (i) defining normalization rules and a fixed taxonomy
(Table 6, Figure 8), (ii) using two independent annotators,
and (iii) resolving disagreements via adjudication. Inter-rater
agreement on a 30% stratified sample reached κ = 0.83
(V-A), indicating strong consistency. Residual risk remains
for edge cases and cross-level terms despite normalization.
2) EVALUATION LEAKAGE AND PROMPT SENSITIVITY

RAG generation can be sensitive to retrieval pool composition
and prompt phrasing. We controlled this with a fixed retriever
index, frozen prompts, and ablations against BM25 and
Embed-only (Table 5). Still, minor variance due to prompt
phrasing and temperature cannot be completely eliminated.
B. EXTERNAL VALIDITY
1) GENERALIZABILITY ACROSS ORGANIZATIONS AND
DOMAINS

The corpus comprises 84 peer-reviewed studies spanning
2016–2025 from major libraries, which helps diversity but
excludes non-English/gray literature. Section VI-G reports
deployments in software platform, banking, and insurance
settings, suggesting applicability at multiple scales; however, regulated environments (e.g., finance/insurance) impose
change-window and compliance constraints that may dampen
or delay some gains. Results should therefore be generalized
with attention to domain constraints and governance maturity.
2) CONCEPT DRIFT

DevOps/MSA terminology evolves (e.g., supply-chain security, platform engineering). The current index is a timebounded snapshot;periodic re-indexing is needed to preserve
external validity as practices change.

D. CONCLUSION VALIDITY
1) STATISTICAL STRENGTH AND INFERENCE LIMITS

The evaluation used 50 curated queries (10 per category).
Results in Table 5 show consistent gains for RAG over baselines, but the sample size constrains statistical power and
per-category subgroup analysis. Human relevance judgments
(Table 2)—although double-annotated—remain partly subjective. Future replications with larger query sets and additional assessors (including industry experts) would further
stabilize estimates.
2) ABLATION COVERAGE

We reported BM25 and Embed-only as baselines; stronger
conclusions would benefit from adding rerankers or hybrid
pipelines. That said, observed improvements are coherent with qualitative inspections and category-level deltas
(Figures 5–7).
E. DATASET AND ANNOTATION LIMITATIONS
1) SELECTION BIAS AND COVERAGE

Inclusion criteria (English, peer-reviewed, 2016–2025)
enhance quality but omit non-English and practice reports.
Industry-heavy topics may be over-represented relative to
niche academic threads.
2) ANNOTATION SUBJECTIVITY AND OVERLAP

Manual extraction can miss implicit concepts or misclassify
overlapping terms. We applied normalization, exemplars, and
adjudication; nonetheless, perfect semantic consistency is
unattainable. Table 9 illustrates how evidence strength varies
by method, reinforcing the need to separate validated from
conceptual mappings (Tables 7–8).
3) STATIC SNAPSHOT

As new practices emerge (e.g., SBOM/SLSA, platform engineering patterns), the index must be refreshed; otherwise, recommendations may gradually misalign with state-of-practice.
F. REPRODUCIBILITY AND TRANSPARENCY

We improve reproducibility by (i) fixing retrieval indices
and prompts for the 50-query run, (ii) publishing taxonomy
definitions and labeling rubrics (referenced in V-A/V-E),
and (iii) reporting κ, per-category metrics, and examples
(Tables 2, 5; Figures 5–7). We encourage third-party replication with expanded query pools, additional annotators,
and domain-specific corpora (e.g., finance/insurance) to test
portability.

VIII. CONCLUSION AND FUTURE WORK

This paper presented an empirical and AI-supported approach
for aligning DevOps practices with microservice architecture
(MSA) principles. From a curated corpus of 84 peer-reviewed
sources (2016–2025), we extracted and normalized concepts,
constructed more than 200 DevOps↔MSA mappings, and
organized them into a five-category taxonomy: Deployment
Automation & Orchestration, Observability & Runtime Diagnostics, Scalability & Modularity, Security & Governance,
and Configuration & Environment Management. We operationalized these insights through a retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) framework that couples sentence-transformer
retrieval with LLM reasoning to recommend bidirectional concept mappings with concise, evidence-grounded
justifications.
Quantitatively, RAG outperformed BM25 and Embed-only
across standard IR metrics (Table 5), and qualitatively its
explanations aligned with human assessments (Table 2).
The empirical synthesis (Tables 7–9; Figures 5–7) shows
that DeploymentAutomation and Observability are the most
strongly validated areas, while Security & Governance
remains comparatively under-validated, indicating a clear
research opportunity. Multiple industrial deployments—
including a software platform, a tier-1 bank, and a national
insurance group—suggest the framework’s practical utility at
different scales and regulatory contexts, with variations that
reflect domain emphasis (Section VI-G).
Overall, the study contributes: (i) a reusable, normalized
concept dataset and taxonomy; (ii) a RAG-based decision
aid that improves architecture-to-operations traceability; and
(iii) an empirical map that highlights where evidence is
strong versus emerging. Taken together, these artifacts help
researchers structure comparative studies and help practitioners plan, justify, and trace DevOps/MSA decisions.
A. FUTURE WORK

1. Broader and fresher evidence. Expand the corpus
to non-English and gray literature; add sector-specific
corpora (e.g., finance, telecom, public sector) and periodically refresh the index to address concept drift (e.g.,
SBOM/SLSA, platform engineering, policy-as-code).
2. Stronger evaluations. Enlarge the query set, include
additional annotators (industry experts), and add
hybrid/reranking baselines and task-level evaluations
(e.g., downstream change-failure rate/MTTR impacts).
3. Methodological
enhancements.
Incorporate
uncertainty-aware retrieval, prompt-robust generation,
and explanation auditing to further strengthen construct
and conclusion validity.

4. Toolchain integration. Package the framework as an
open, extensible toolkit that plugs into CI/CD platforms
and architecture workbenches to provide in-flow recommendations, traceability links (mapping → change
→ outcome), and governance reports.
5. Education and training. Use the taxonomy and
dataset to design curricula and interactive labs that connect DevOps practices to microservice design concerns
with explainable, evidence-based exercises.
By releasing the dataset, taxonomy, and implementation artifacts, we aim to catalyze replication and extension. We see empirically grounded RAG as a practical
path toward evidence-informed, auditable decisions that
bridge architecture and operations in complex software
systems.
